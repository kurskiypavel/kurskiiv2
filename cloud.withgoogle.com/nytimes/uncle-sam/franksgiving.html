Franksgiving

The annual Macy’s Thanksgiving Day Parade, the world’s largest parade, is presented by the U.S. department store chain Macy’s. The parade began in 1924.

DBPedia

U.S. National Archives

Starting with Abraham Lincoln 76 years earlier, U.S. Presidents had declared a day of Thanksgiving to be observed on the last Thursday of each November.

NATIONAL ARCHIVES CATALOG

"Club men of Columbus in caricature", W.A. Ireland, H.J. Westerman, Ray Rohn, Fair Use

Three months prior to the photo, at the urging of Macy’s Chairman Fred Lazarus Jr., President Franklin D. Roosevelt broke custom and announced that Thanksgiving would occur a week earlier. It was thought that a longer Christmas shopping season would help the nation’s economy during the Great Depression.

Google Books

Franklin D. Roosevelt Library

The change affected holiday plans for millions of Americans and led to widespread confusion. 23 states and the District of Columbia recognized the new date, 22 states kept the traditional date, and three states observed both dates.

Google Books + DBpedia

Image from The Indiana Gazette

The holiday was regarded by many as Franksgiving — a combination of "Franklin" and “Thanksgiving.” Two years later, in 1941, a Commerce Department survey found no significant expansion of holiday retail sales due to the change. Congress then passed a joint resolution, signed by Roosevelt, making the fourth Thursday in November the legal holiday.
